Content Brief

Learning from Experience, Planning for the Possible

By Gary Arlen
November 19, 2010
 
"When Media Are New" is not just an entrancing book title. It's also a great reminder to content and technology developers about what they should understand during the pioneering days as they introduce devices and applications in the ever-evolving "new media" environment. With annotated checklists such as "the potential role of seemingly small chance effects in determining the fate of a new product" this impressive book provides a perceptive look into the recent past and a visionary view of what's ahead.

Although "When Media Are New" is a scholarly volume, its approach focuses on real-world business examples. The professors/authors, John Carey and Martin Elton, have conducted countless field studies for media and electronics companies, seeking to understand how and why consumers embrace or disdain content via online networks, satellite radio, interactive TV and, videophones among a half-dozen systems examined in the "case studies" half of the book.

Their real-world experience shines through in perceptions such as this: "One of the most important characteristics of the current media environment is not technological but social. It is the expectation that media and content should be available on demand almost anywhere."

While that call to "control, convenience and customization of the TV experience" may seem intuitive today, Carey and Elton provide the three-decade background details to demonstrate how the industries came to understand that relationship. They also make it vividly clear that TV will maintain a central role in Americans' lives, no matter what delivery systems are deployed.

"Regardless of changes in technology, television's core functions of providing relaxation, entertainment, information and escape are unlikely to change," Carey and Elton assert. Acknowledging that "the ways in which these core functions are provided will change," the professors contend that "viewing behavior and programming will continue to evolve" to meet those tech developments.

Their book is a fitting complement to "When Old Technologies Were New," a 20-year old volume that examined the long-term impact of "innovations" such as electricity and telephones. Carey and Elton recognize that "new media" now face some of the same challenges as "old technologies," including price barriers and government intervention (regulation/legislation). They also emphasize that the innovations from all directions - giant firms and entrepreneurial ventures - add to the complexity for infrastructure developers and end users alike.

The range of these complexities was underscored by this week's news headlines: "When Media Are New." Intel's $77 million investment in dozens of ventures put money into several companies that support "new media" in the broadest sense. Intel's funding is focused on companies that "spur innovation" in technologies that rely on computer processors. Among the latest recipients of Intel's funding are Verismo Networks, which is developing IP video, social networks and personal media on TV, and YuMe, which delivers video ads across online, mobile and CE/TV devices.

As Carey and Elton point out, timing is everything. A couple of widely publicized research reports that surfaced last week reinforced the idea that viewers are maintaining their TV habits. The "Multiplatform Content and Services Study" from Horowitz Associates showed a significant jump in the number of young people (ages 18 to 34) who watch TV content online or on handheld devices: 39 percent this year compared to 34 percent a year ago.

"The penetration and usage of alternative viewing technology is reaching a tipping point and the measurable impact on how the TV product is viewed will inevitably follow," says Howard Horowitz, president of Horowitz Associates.

Separately, a Nielsen study for the Cable & Telecommunications Association for Marketing (CTAM) found that alleged "cord-cutters" are actually watching the same amount or more of regularly scheduled TV than they were before. They are just opting to see it as streamed content via the Internet. The study found that 84 percent of people who watch Internet video on their TV sets tune into the same content they watch via cable. Only three percent of viewers say they will actually give up their cable subscriptions altogether.

The Horowitz and Nielsen research offers good, new snapshots of viewing behavior. The Carey/Elton book provides historic perspective, with interpretations applicable to today's new media environment. For content creators as well as infrastructure distributors, the challenge remains: how to bring these perceptions together into a business model. At CES, the two-day conference "Media Money Makers" will address these issues. The agenda includes the latest thinking from studios and independent content developers, matched with the business perspective of network and technology gatekeepers. Collectively, they are constructing the newest new media lessons that Carey and Elton - or their academic successors - will chronicle and evaluate in the decades ahead.

Gary Arlen is President of Arlen Communications Inc., a Bethesda, MD, research firm; he is co-producer of Media Money Makers, a conference on media monetization in the digital era, at the 2011 International CES on January 6-7.
