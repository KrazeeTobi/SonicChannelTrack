Cricket’s MUVE and Sony’s Qriocity Music Opens Way for Mobile Music to Change Its Tune


By Gary Arlen
December 29, 2010
 

The almost simultaneous debuts of two mobile music subscription ventures – Cricket Communications’ “Muve Music” and Sony’s “Music Unlimited powered by Qriocity” – underscore not only the vast untapped revenue potential in the entertainment market. The late-December transatlantic announcements are also reminders that the biggest media and wireless providers are still tinkering with the business models that will further expand this vast category. Their plans also demonstrate the diverse approaches that are being explored – configurations of bundled fees, ad support and subscription options.


“Muve Music” packages unlimited music access into the flat $55 monthly fee for Cricket’s new voice and data service ($10 more than Cricket’s non-music package). Sony’s music service, which initially offers access to six million songs, is starting in England and Ireland; it is due to come to the U.S. in 2011. The European prices are equivalent to $6 per month for a basic ad-free package and $15 equivalent monthly for a “premium service” that lets users create playlists and access 100 specialty channels.


Obviously both Cricket – with its four large music partners – and Sony are pushing their alternatives to iTunes; they also believe that there are demographic categories not fully served by Apple’s music options. In particular, Cricket Wireless, a mobile carrier available in 35 U.S. states, expects that its flat-fee bundle of unlimited music downloads, unlimited voice calling, texting, e-mail and Web access will appeal to middle-income and ethnic audiences who have not yet acquired smartphones. Cricket has collaborated with Samsung, which is providing its new “Suede” handset for $199 customized for the Muve service. Samsung’s SCH-R710 is not actually a smartphone, but rather a feature phone with a 3-inch touch screen and a dedicated music button to showcase media content.


Sony’s package is part of its on-demand Qriocity venture, which will include video and apps for multiple platforms. Initially, the music service will be available on network-enabled Bravia TV sets, Blu-ray Disc players, PlayStation3 consoles and computers. Sony says that eventually Qriocity will reach other Sony portable devices (including Sony Ericsson handsets) and Android-based mobile devices.


Cricket and Sony are expected to offer additional details about their services at CES. Executives of many of the participating companies will speak at the “Muve Music from Cricket: The Business Model,” the final session of the Media Money Makers conference on Friday, January 7, at 4 pm in Las Vegas Convention Center Room N250. Among the panelists are Doug Hutcheson, President/CEO, Cricket Communications; Michael Nash, Executive VP, Digital Strategy & Business Development, Warner Music Group; Michael Paull, Executive VP Global Digital Business, Sony Music Entertainment; Matt Peterson, Director of Mobile Content for Samsung Mobile; and Rob Wells, President of Global Digital Business, Universal Music Group.


As new mobile rivals to Apple’s iconic iTunes service, Cricket and Sony are taking approaches that raise many questions. There are fundamental issues such as the depth of the music libraries. (Sony’s initial six million tracks is less than half the size of the iTunes library) and digital rights management (Qriocity puts a “digital fingerprint” on each song).


The downloading versus streaming factor is still in play. (Muve is strictly a download service, which better leverages the capabilities of Cricket’s network. Sony’s service streams songs to the handset.)


DRM issues also persist: Muve lets subscribers keep downloaded tracks when they changes phones. Cricket uses an encrypted partition rather than a DRM system in response to music industry demand for content protection. The Sony digital fingerprint has been criticized as limiting if users want to move song collections to other devices.


And there’s always the pricing issue. Previous stand-alone subscription ventures floundered, but Cricket and Sony contend that their new approaches draw on lessons learned from Real Networks, Napster and other services. Advertising and other marketing options are still being explored. The paltry $44 million ad revenue per year (according to eMarketer) that music services now generate means that there’s an immense upside.


As the Los Angeles Times points out, this new breed of music services – especially Muve – is being “designed from scratch as a service for phones, optimized for a mobile network.” For the music companies, the new iTunes alternatives shift the focus from revenue per transaction (song purchases) to revenue per user, as Warner Music’s Nash explains.


Now we have to see if the music industry’s monetization model is really going to change its tune.




Gary Arlen is President of Arlen Communications Inc., a Bethesda, MD, research firm; he is co-producer of Media Money Makers, a conference on media monetization in the digital era, at the 2011 International CES on January 6-7.
