Content Brief

Changing the Way Things Work Again. Then Again. And Again.

By Gary Arlen
January 28, 2011
 
Netflix' recent announcements - the ABC/Disney distribution deal on the heels of its $7.99 per month streaming package – brutally remind us that it's hard to make long-term plans in this business. Similarly, two Google developments that surfaced a day apart – although widely diverse – add to perceived constant and seismic changes in the content business.

In the same vein, YouTube's recent decision to eliminate its 15-minute limit on video lengths is a double-edge change: it allow more sitcom-length programs onto its platform, and also opens the door for longer Google-owned content, such as shows that might become available via a proposed acquisition (see below). Google says that the change was made possible in part by continued advances in its state-of-the-art Content ID system, which includes "powerful tools" for copyright owners.

One keen observer of the media/tech scene is Alan Wurtzel, president of research and media development at NBC Universal. He recognizes what the constant overhaul is doing to business, and he singles out "the dynamic pace of change" as having a dramatic effect.

"Things are just happening so quickly," Wurtzel observes. "New devices are coming out and mainstreaming very quickly."

"People are using media the same or even more than they did in the past. But even though we call it mass media, they're [watching] it across many, many different platforms," he adds.

Wurtzel expounded on these issues at a session called "Who's Really Watching What and How" during the "Media Money Makers" conference at CES.

Today's frenetic changes in the media and technology landscape may not come as quickly today as they did in the almost-daily quake during the boom a decade ago. Nonetheless the current fast evolution makes it hard for legacy companies to commit to platforms and devices, never certain that tomorrow's developments will again force them to revise their thinking and their investments.

Critics pick on the big companies for using such evolutionary changes as a reason not to make commitments. The constant anticipation of new capabilities forces media leaders to respond more quickly than ever, a process that does not necessarily come naturally to them.

Hence it's easy to understand why content providers say they must move cautiously. For example, a recent forecast by In-Stat strongly insists that video downloads and streaming revenues will triple in the next three years while DVD retail sales will see double-digit declines. That puts everyone on pause for a moment.

If the forecast is accurate, what's the ripple effect on everyone involved in the shrink-wrap value chain? Separate comments from home video insiders add to the uncertainty: TV shows on DVD face a perilous future as Hulu, Netflix and other TV archive services expand their libraries. You can expect the TV-on-DVD structure to change again several times in coming years, albeit in a slow, measured process.

Sometimes the changes involve a step backward for every two steps forward. Google revealed in December that it will postpone its field trial of an ultra high-speed delivery system: 1 Gbps (gigabit per second), which would put Google in competition with legacy carriers while dwarfing telco and cable speeds. A day later, reports surfaced that Google (through its YouTube subsidiary) is negotiating to acquire Next New Networks, a video production venture that has encouraged high-quality, short-form shows and series such as "Barely Political" and "Glitter Puke."

One underpinning of the Google/Next New alliance is that it gives Google – an evolving media company itself – "green light" capabilities. A low-cost production from Next New's roster can be audience-tested online as a TV pilot or movie trailer. Shows that get good responses could be "upgraded" to mass productions.

This continual revision of the media landscape will never end. The speed may change but no one is quite sure if it will continue to get faster, or if there will be some slowdown as all sectors try to catch their breath.

Wurtzel himself is still on the lookout for game-changing developments, such as mobile devices.

Like others, he found much at CES that was brand new, and likely to be outdated a year from now.



Gary Arlen is President of Arlen Communications Inc., a Bethesda, MD, research firm; he is co-producer of Media Money Makers, a conference on media monetization in the digital era, at the 2011 International CES on January 6-7.
