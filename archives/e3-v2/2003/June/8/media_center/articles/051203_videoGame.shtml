Dow Jones Newswires, May 12, 2003

Video Game Cos. Quiet On Price Plans Before Industry Show
By Steven Vames, Dow Jones Newswires

LOS ANGELES — With just days to go before the video game industry's annual confab in Los Angeles, it's anybody's guess whether the three major hardware makers are going to cut retail prices of their machines.

Ahead of this year's Electronic Entertainment Exposition, or E3 , the three hardware makers are being especially tight-lipped about if, and by how much, they will cut prices on their machines, which include Sony Corp.'s (SNE) Playstation2, Microsoft Corp.'s (MSFT) Xbox and Nintendo Corp.'s (J.NTD) GameCube.

For hardware makers, E3 , which starts Wednesday, is generally the key time for announcing price cuts. Last year, for example, with Xbox and GameCube having been introduced only months earlier, the specter of competition caused all three makers to reduce prices to current levels.

"Last year, word leaked out early and we all knew what was going to happen. This year, they've been very quiet," says Michael Wallace, analyst at UBS Warburg. Wallace doesn't own stock in any of the three game makers and UBS Warburg has done - or intends to do - banking for Sony and Microsoft.

On the BestBuy.com retail Web site Friday, Playstation2 was listed at $199.99, GameCube was listed at $149.99 and Xbox was listed at $199.99. Those were the suggested retail prices set last year at E3.

This time around the stakes are different. Playstation2 has emerged as the clear dominant player in the sector, with an estimated 70% market share, versus 15% each for the others, according to Wallace. With Sony ahead of the pack and continuing to outsell the others, analysts say it's up in the air whether the company needs to increase the incentive for buying its machine.

"All you can really say at this point is that there is a 50-50 chance of a price cut on Playstation2," says Edward Williams, analyst at Gerard Klauer Mattison. "On one hand a price cut could occur because it could boost demand, but on the other hand, Sony is already tracking a robust hardware sales figure, and are within shot of their 10 million unit goal."

Williams does not own Sony stock and it was unclear Friday whether Gerard Klauer has a banking relationship with the company.

Sony would not comment on their plan for pricing, but Kaz Hirai, president of the Playstation2 unit, says he is "comfortable with our position, and we feel like we can chart our course by where we feel like we should be, rather than being influenced by external factors."

Xbox Seen Most Likely To Cut Price

Analysts put the biggest odds of a price cut on Xbox. The system has met Microsoft's sales expectations last year and gained some traction against Sony and Nintendo, but the company has lowered sales growth projections for this year. Still, analysts note that Microsoft remains heavily committed to Xbox, despite the fact it is known to be losing money on each unit it sells, hoping to recoup losses by producing software, licensing third-party software and offering subscription-based online gaming.

Last month, Microsoft cut the price of Xbox in Europe for the second time since it was introduced in March 2002, lowering it to EUR199.99 from EUR249.99, following a May 2002 cut from EUR299.99. In the U.S. it has only cut the price once, at the 2002 E3 conference, to its current $199.99 from its introductory price of $299.99 in March 2002.

"A cut in the price of Xbox is the most widely anticipated move on the hardware front," said Dan Gardenswartz, principle at the Sage Group, a Los Angeles-based investment bank. "There is a lot of talk about a $150 price point for Xbox."

Software Bundling Vs Price Cuts

Alternately, says Gardenswartz, the gaming companies might be satisfied to offer more for the same price by "bundling" software or hardware with their machines.

There is some speculation, for example, that Sony might bundle its $40 Network Adapter, which allows for online gaming, with Playstation2. Xbox comes standard with a networking capability and GameCube doesn't offer an online capability.

Nintendo reported some success with an ongoing promotion in which it gives away a free game from a select list of titles with the purchase of a GameCube. Microsoft has said that last year's holiday offer, which included a new controller and two games for $199.99, helped it's holiday sales figures.

But it's unclear whether software bundling will have much of an effect on sales, and analysts remain skeptical.

"Both Nintendo and Microsoft have proven that bundling games doesn't have much of a lasting effect on sales or market share," said Shawn Milne, analyst at Soundview Technology group.

"From the retailer's perspective, volume historically picks up at lower price points," rather than adding goods at the same price, said Milne. "We'll just have to see what happens this time around."

Milne does not own any of the console-makers' stock, and Soundview has not done banking with any of them.
