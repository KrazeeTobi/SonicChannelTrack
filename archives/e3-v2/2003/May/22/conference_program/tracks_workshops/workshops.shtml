Workshops
E3 workshops offer a unique opportunity to learn what it takes to succeed in today's competitive computer game industry. Expose yourself to some of the industry's most talented - and experienced - professionals as they discuss elements of design, market research, and promotion. Learn from experts and apply that knowledge to your own game development or publishing enterprise

W1 - Read the Marketplace for Added Profit
Tuesday, May 13, 2003, 9:30 AM - 11:30 AM
Room: 411
 
How does a consumer end up buying a game? What are the steps or stages s/he goes through in making a purchase? Learn how the industry makes wise use of market research, in-game player metrics, focus groups, and fourth-party developers to create and support a top-selling title. Come away from this session with an understanding of the modern tools behind the blockbuster hits, the role that pre-release research can play in influencing development ideas, and how you can tap some of those techniques for your own.

Moderator:   Michael Gartenberg, Vice President and Research Director, Jupiter Research
Panelists:   Carolyn Feinstein, Vice President of Marketing Communications, Electronic Arts
Bill Fulton, User Testing Manager, Microsoft
Scott McDaniel, Vice President Marketing & PR, Sony Online Entertainment
Tobi Saulnier, VP, Product Development, Vicarious Visions

W2 - The Design Document: Necessary, Necessary Evil, or Not Needed?
Tuesday, May 13, 2003, 9:30 AM - 11:30 AM
Room: 404AB
 
A widely contested strategy in game production concerns the design document and to what extent it should rule the design process. Understood by all as an essential tool for effective planning, debate concerning ways to implement it differs greatly. This workshop will look at all sides of the argument and address how to create a game design document and how to follow it. Learn under what conditions it should be deviated from and how it best fits into the grand scheme of project planning. Hear from a panel representing MMOL, console, PC, and handheld platforms, and come away understanding how a DD can work both for you, and against you.

Moderator:   Randy Angle, President/Creative Director, Pronto Games
Panelists:   Haden Blackman, Producer, LucasArts Entertainment
Todd Howard, Executive Producer, Bethesda Softworks
Marc LeBlanc, Software Engineer, Visual Concepts
Carly Staehlin, Design Manager, NCsoft

W3 - Smart Games Break Out: The Global Scene
Tuesday, May 13, 2003, 9:30 AM - 11:30 AM
Room: 406AB
 
Out of the simulation genre has come a number of games designed to engage gamers in fixing, training, and learning. Smart Games are the logical result of the transfer of ideas and technology that has toggled between the entertainment, military, and academic communities over the past 20 years. This session will bring together a group of committed businesspeople from around the world and ask them to address their success on the front lines of Smart Games: hear how games can train first responders in a bio-terrorism attack, how a MMOL game in Korea can be built to be both fun and educational, find out what’s behind the funding initiatives of one the world’s largest corporations, and hear first hand how the field of computer-generated agents for use in e- Commerce and adaptive training environments is progressing. Walk away from this session with an understanding of how games are being used for training and education within the medical, academic, and business sectors. See how Smart Games are becoming a growing niche, and learn how you can participate in its growth.

Moderator:   Jesse Schell, Professor Entertainment Technology, Carnegie Mellon Univ.
Panelists:   Barbara Hayes-Roth, CEO, Extempo Systems
Randy Hinrichs, Group Program Mgr. for Learning Science and Tech, Microsoft
Chong-Heon Kim (Jay Kim), VP, Interesting & Creative (KOREA: Demiurges team)
Michael Macedonia, CTO, U.S.Army
Seul-Ki Park, Project Manager, Daekyo (KOREA: Demiurges team)

W4 - Working Smart: Understanding the Decision-Making Process at the Highest Levels
Tuesday, May 13, 2003, 1:00 PM - 3:00 PM
Room: 411
 
How do major developers and publishers approach the game production process? What criteria determine whether a game gets green-lighted or shot down when first pitched? How do decision- makers determine a game’s budget, the size of the development team, the production schedule, and best strategy to roll out the product in foreign markets? How do the top publishers and developers deal with delays in production, changing consumers’ tastes, or unexpected glitches in the process? This panel will ask some of the industry’s biggest players to discuss how a game goes from idea to finished code. Hear the stories behind Harry Potter, the Oddworld legacy, and Neverwinter Nights. Be better at your business by hearing how the biggest players in our industry approach the full spectrum of project approval and management.

Moderator:   Aaron Loeb, Producer, Planet Moon Studios

W5 - Use the Web to Publish, Market, and Protect Your Game
Tuesday, May 13, 2003, 1:00 PM - 3:00 PM
Room: 404AB
 
The number of online gamers is expected to continue to grow -- just as the quality of web- delivered games is expected to improve. So what are the opportunities for the creative indie development house? And how are the traditional players expected to take advantage of new opportunities the web presents? This panel of experienced players in the demo/download, browser game, casual game, and digital delivery space will address the new rules of development, distribution, and delivery. Walk away knowing what content is selling, what the best technologies for the product are, and how deals between independent studios and game aggregators are structured.

W6 - Pitching Your Game Idea or Studio's Expertise: The New Rules
Tuesday, May 13, 2003, 1:00 PM - 3:00 PM
Room: 406AB
 
These days, you don't just prep your pitch for the traditional publisher community. Often, you are pitching a subset of your studio's capabilities, or you may find yourself pitching to an investment firm or outside production company. W7 - Persistent World Overview: Where the Bar is Now?
Tuesday, May 13, 2003, 3:30 PM - 5:30 PM
Room: 411
 
Developers and publishers are diving into the PSW market at a rapid pace. So what are the main challenges facing the PSW development studio today? What are the costs of producing a MMOL title? What are the technical hurdles? How do you best prepare your live team? Our panel will discuss the content, tech, and pre- and post- launch attributes that separate the good from the bad among persistent world titles. One of the most influential and respected producers in online gaming leads a panel of both experienced hands and MMOL novices in a discussion of what’s required today to be a success as a persistent world developer or publisher.

W8 - Funding Tip Sheet: Understand the New Money Stream
Tuesday, May 13, 2003, 3:30 PM - 5:30 PM
Room: 404AB
 
The funding environment continues to evolve: From funding game development, to working with licensed IPs, new rules are in play. Our panel -- from the investment, game development, and the IP ownership and licensing worlds -- will discuss what funding models have traction among investors today, what publishers are funding, how you can best package your business plan or game idea for maximum upside in the funding stage, and recent developments in the area where games and other entertainment media (including TV and film) meet. Come away with an understanding of who is funding original concepts (even when publishers are not), exactly what investors are looking for from a game company and game idea, and what new models of risk mitigation and alternate funding are taking hold.
