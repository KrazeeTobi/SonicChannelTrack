Some points to be considered regarding "small-bumps" toys




(1) Some special features for the sight-impaired:

The sight-impaired can play with any game or toy after touching them with their hands and checking sounds.
Accordingly, it is requested that special attention be paid to the touch and hearing of these people from the planning and development stages of any new products.


[Major points to be considered]

1. In the case of toys equipped with batteries:

the cap position of the battery container and the method of opening it may be confirmed by touch.

the direction in which the batteries are inserted into the container may be confirmed by touch.

2. In the case of toys equipped with switches:

the conditions of the switch may be confirmed by touch.

attachment of "small-bumps" indication on "ON" position of switch.

installing a sound device which is triggered whenever a switch is turned "ON".

3. In the case of toys which must be distinguished by color:

In games or toys which must be distinguished by color, not less than two things may be distinguished through touch and sound other than by color.

4. In the case of movable toys:

The position of the toy may be confirmed by sound, whenever the toy moves out of the child's reach so that the movement of the toy may be controlled, however far away the toy may be.

5. Other points:

Toys which are copied from an actual object are required to resemble it as much as possible in shape and feel.

The process and results of the play should be understandable without requiring tability to see.

Toys must not be easily broken or made faulty by touch alone.

Toys must be attached with sealed braille, if necessary.

Toys must be manufactured so that normal people can enjoy them even with their eyes closed.

* Please refer to the guidelines for toys manufactured both for normal people and the sight-impaired for more detailed information.


(2) Some special features for the hearing-impaired:

The hearing-impaired, as opposed to the sight-impaired, can play with any soundless toys with no problem.
However, any other toys than those producing no sound have to be paid careful attention to during the development of the toys in order to be enjoyed by both normal and hard-hearing people.


[Major points to be considered]

Some points to be considered about co-playable toys which can be enjoyed with the hearing-impaired are now under internal discussion and scheduled to be published in the guidelines for fiscal 1996.
However, please consult with the secretariat of The Japan Toy Association, if you have any interest in or questions about the toys in the following categories:

1. Toys designed for pleasant communication:

It is very important for children to enjoy playing together with their friends.
However, hearing-impaired children sometimes have difficulty when they cannot communicate smoothly while playing with games or toys which employ sounds and voices with their playmates.

On these occasions, if they exchange signals using communication methods other than sound or voice, they will be able to enjoy playing pleasantly together in a group.
And in order to enjoy the communication itself with each other, a board on which they can write letters or words for conversation by writing or a letter telephone through which they can exchange letters mutually from a distance are of great value for promotion of enjoyable communication among the hearing-impaied.

2. Toys with other methods of stimulation like light, vibration, movement, letters, pictures, etc. other than sound:

having adjustable sound functions from the highest to the lowest volume;

having sound functions from the highest to the lowest pitch;

having an earphone terminal; and

producing sound which is clearly recognizable

3. Toys whose part names are indicated with printed letters or seals.

4. Toys which are attached with sign-language seals

5. Toys in which the speaker's voice can be shown by lights, vibration, movements, letters, pictures, etc.

6. Toys which employ the breath or tongue

7. Toys which can effectively teach the public how inconvenient it is to be hearing-impaired
