Converging Technologies Anchor 1997 Winter CES®


Growth of Industry and Show Reflected in Wireless/Cellular, Satellite,
Accessories and Home Systems Categories


ARLINGTON, VIRGINIA, October 8, 1996. If the science fiction writers scribing earlier in this century had their way, consumers would probably be floating in air ships, hovercrafting their way to work, and creating elaborate meals out of a seed the size of a speck of dust.

Although consumers are floating in air ships (they're called airplanes) and creating elaborate meals out of seemingly nothing -- thanks to two-family households and busy lifestyles -- they're hardly doing much hovercrafting. But the very idea that anyone could accomplish the heretofore unimaginable -- a science fiction staple -- and create these previously unimaginable things is being practiced day in, day out, by the consumer electronics industry.

At the 1997 International Winter Consumer Electronics Showr (CES ® ), being held in Las Vegas this coming January 9th through 12th, the convergence of a variety of technologies that result in the creation of products that enhance the quality of the lives of millions of consumers around the world will be in the spotlight.

More than 1,500 companies -- 234 new exhibitors -- are set to be in place exhibiting on the show floors this coming January. Nearly one million square feet of exhibit space has already been assigned. With a focus on converging technology-infused products, and a wide array of manufacturers from the United States and such countries as France, Germany, Hong Kong, Israel, Italy, Korea, Nigeria, and Taiwan, the 1997 Winter CES is shaping up to be another strong consumer electronics showcase.

Also converging in Las Vegas this year is a variety of hot topics being addressed in a comprehensive conference program: over 50 sessions are being held at all four properties housing the 1997 Winter CES (the Alexis Park Hotel, Las Vegas Convention Center (LVCC), Sands Expo and Convention Center and the Las Vegas Hilton). The sessions, free to all badged-attendees, are comprised of eight tracks. These tracks include new technology, internet, multimedia, SoHo, specialty audio, mobile electronics, home theater and general sessions. Two other special six-session accredited tracks on retail management and home systems are available for $125 or $30 a session.

In addition, Michael Bloomberg, president and founder of Bloomberg Financial Markets, will deliver the 1997 Winter CES keynote address in the LVCC on January 9th, along with Bob Borchardt, president of Recoton and Gary Shapiro, president of the Consumer Electronics Manufacturers Association (CEMA), sponsor of the CES.

Convergence is certainly a buzzword in today's consumer electronics universe. "We hear a great deal about convergence these days," says Shapiro. "Witness products being created for use with the Internet, such as PC TVs or web TVs, or for use in home offices, such as all-in-one fax/copier/scanner units. 'Converging technologies' are key to these and other products' success. In fact, certain products like these would not be possible if a variety of technologies did not converge."

A quick look at the names of some exhibitors showing products anchored by converging technologies tells the tale: Casio, Denon, Thomson, Philips, Pioneer, Sharp, Sony, and Thomson. Clearly, convergence is a 1997 Winter CES benchmark.

A first-time Winter CES Emerging Technologies Pavilion, located on the lower level of the Sands, will zero in on the efforts of small companies manufacturing products infused by new and exciting technologies. Eligible companies are those who produce less than $2 million in revenue, have never had a booth larger than 10' x 10' at a CES, and have fewer than 15 employees. The Pavilion is ideal for companies that are looking to gain exposure to nearly 80,000 financiers, investors, venture capitalists, retailers and trade press. The pavilion is a veritable one-stop-shopping experience for 1997 Winter CES attendees.

Exhibitors Embrace Winter CES

As competition for consumers' disposable income continues to increase, it is becoming more important for consumer electronics manufacturers to target their products to the trade at the Winter CES. Square footage for specific product areas is also on the upswing; most notably, gainers include cellular and wireless equipment, SoHo, home systems, accessories and satellite categories. A steady increase is also visible in mobile electronics, home theater and furniture.

All of this and more will be played out on the show floors in all 1997 Winter CES properties. Responding to changes in the consumer electronics marketplace, the show floors have been rearranged to serve attendees better.

Sands Expo and Convention Center

Much is happening at the Sands Expo and Convention Center. Mobile electronics manufacturers will join those of the cellular, paging, wireless, telephone and personal electronics varieties there. All but the personal electronics companies will be located on the hotel's upper level in the A, B and C halls; the rest will reside on the lower level of the hotel's convention center in hall G. Over 160,000 square feet of mobile electronics space has been reserved. "It was important to move the mobile electronics exhibitors to a location that would accommodate this product categories' growth," says Jonathan Thompson, CES vice president.

The Winter CES is important to Earthquake Sound Corporation. Eddie Chucrallah, operations manager, says the company believes "that the Winter CES is the medium of choice to project our message to current and future clients. Facing and interacting with clients on a one-to-one basis is very important to us -- what better way to do it than at CES. For us, the CES is a 'monstrous' display for new products and innovative ideas, whether (they are) Earthquake's or someone else's."

Chucrallah also says that the benefits of the CES "extend beyond the U.S. continent. A large part of Earthquake's international business was acquired at CES, and we are 'shooting' for much more. Earthquake runs massive advertisement campaigns in trade magazines, yet we always rely on CES to acquire more business."

Overall, the Sands represents more than 500,000 square feet of exhibit space: approximately 650 exhibitors have already been assigned space here. In other words, the Sands is truly a critical mix of the show. In addition to substantial exhibit space, the Sands will also house Innovations '97, the annual showcase of design, engineering and software excellence in the consumer electronics marketplace. The Innovations '97 Design & Engineering Honors Program is endorsed by the Industrial Designers Society of America.

Other key exhibitors in the Sands include cellular/wireless manufacturers like Motorola (5,000 square feet), Ericsson (4,000 square feet), and Samsung Cellular (3,000 square feet). Nokia, PageMart, PageNet, and Pacific Bell Mobile Services will also be present, as well as caller ID manufacturers CIDCO and Colonial Data.

Companies such as PageMart Wireless consider CES to be an important showcase for their products. "The Consumer Electronics Show is one of the most successful trade shows we attend all year," says John D. Beletic, PageMart Wireless's president and CEO. "CES gives us a chance to meet with hundreds of buyers and resellers to discuss new products, services and promotions."

The Sands is also the home of the Intelligent Transportation System (ITS) Pavilion, which will feature products designed to improve passenger and freight efficiency and safety along surface transportation modes. There's a ready-made market for ITS products: 83 percent of Americans are making at least one "road trip" lasting 100 miles or more every year. On display in this pavilion will be in-vehicle navigation systems, traveler information systems, safety devices, mayday and collision notification systems, and vehicle radar systems. Manufacturers represented will include Etak, Navigation Technologies, Rockwell and Siemens.

Specialty Audio moves to the Alexis Park Hotel

There are over 200 exhibitors committed to displaying their products in the new home of specialty audio at the Winter CES -- the Alexis Park Hotel, located just under two miles from the Las Vegas Convention Center (shuttle buses run every 10 minutes). The move to the Alexis Park looks to be a successful and lasting one, as well as a clear representation to the trade of the power of specialty audio.

Exhibitors showing their specialty audio products at the Alexis Park will range from AudioQuest to Vandersteen Audio, from Krell Industries to Thiel, and from Martin-Logan to Mobile Fidelity. Six conference sessions, part of the overall 1997 Winter CES Conference Program, will be held in the hotel. The sessions include "How to Introduce New People to Specialty Audio," "The Art of Listening -- How to Conduct Listening Tests with Demos," and "High-End Accessories -- Do They Make a Difference?"

The Las Vegas Convention Center

The Winter CES's traditional audio and video exhibit areas, located in the LVCC, are thriving once again this year. Sold out for the most part, the LVCC is packed with all manner of audio and video products (on the main and upper levels and in North 1 and the South 2, 3, 4 and 5 halls).

With the LVCC's Grand Lobby retooled to accommodate fire marshal restrictions, many publication and trade show booths will be scattered among the show properties, rather than simply reside in the Grand Lobby of the LVCC where they have traditionally been situated. Although some will remain in the lobby this year, others will be placed by product category in the appropriate location.

On the LVCC floor, don't miss the many new and exciting products on display, like Microsoft's new Windows CE operating system for handheld personal computers, personal digital assistants (PDAs), and other like products (look for it in the company's 1,200 square foot booth in South 2).

With a wide variety of Internet-related products on the market, it might seem as though CES is going "Internet crazy." However, it's just reliable Winter CES magic, bringing manufacturers such as Hughes, Oracle, Philips, Sony, Thomson and Zenith together to reflect the continuing turn of the consumer electronics industry toward all things Internet.

The ever-changing worlds of multimedia and desktop publishing -- both ongoing centers for converging technologies -- will be on display at the Multimedia/Desktop Publishing Pavilion in the LVCC. Expect to see a variety of multimedia hardware and accessories on view, along with CD-ROM-based electronic gaming and software products.

The 1997 Winter CES is also the premier January showcase for satellite products (which will be found in the LVCC). Winter CES is clearly the place for satellite manufacturers to exhibit their products -- satellite square footage rose an incredible 1,100 percent over last year's show. On display will be the latest products from such companies as Echostar Communications, DirecTV, General Instrument and Prime Star.

The LVCC's North 2 hall is the main home for small office/home office, or SoHo, exhibits. The SoHo offerings will come from such companies as Brother International, Franklin Electronic Publishers, Hewlett-Packard USA, Royal Consumer Business Products, and Smith Corona.

Tedd Rozylowicz, vice president in Sharp Electronics' Consumer Business Products Group, says that "The Winter Consumer Electronics Show is an integral part of Sharp's overall marketing program. It's our best opportunity to show our customers the types of products and marketing plans we have for the next year. It also enables our dealers to meet with many levels of our management teams in sales and marketing."

Furniture, the stationary anchor for most consumer electronics products, is making a big splash at the 1997 Winter CES in the South 1 and 2 and 3-5 halls, with manufacturers such as Bush Industries, Gusdorf, and Sauder Woodworking displaying their products.

More LVCC excitement comes in the form of digital cameras from manufacturers such as Casio and Vivitar.

The Las Vegas Hilton is the 1997 Winter CES headquarters for home theater and home systems exhibitors, in which a showcase will be provided for the growing synergy between the two areas. Look for a variety of exciting products to put a charge into the ever-important home theater sector of the consumer electronics industry, including those from Advanced Technology Group; first-time CES exhibitor Prosumer Technologies, (a new division of Extron Electronics being unveiled at the show); first-time Winter CES exhibitor THX, (which will be cross-promoting its licensees who are showing their products in Las Vegas); MB Quart; MTX; and Snell Acoustics.

"As a new division of Extron Electronics, Prosumer Technologies sees Winter CES as a tremendous opportunity to publicize our new line of home theater products to the extensive consumer electronics market," says Gary Kayye, vice president, sales and marketing, Prosumer Technologies.

The Home Systems market, which is experiencing nearly a 70 percent increase at this time in square footage at the 1997 Winter CES and continues to grow in appeal to the retail marketplace, architects and custom installers, will be represented by a number of exhibitors, including Crestron, Lutron Electronics, Phast, and X-10 (USA).

Bringing It All Home

The 1997 Winter CES will be a classic showcase for the consumer electronics industry's new and existing products: from accessories and audio and video hardware and software to small office/home office, specialty audio and telephone products, Las Vegas will be the place to be this coming January.

"We always say that the 'next' Winter CES will be the best ever," says Gary Shapiro. "But it's absolutely true. The industry changes constantly, and with so many new products and trends evident on our show floors each year, it's important for the industry to have a regular, reliable showcase. The 1997 Winter CES is the next 'best ever' showcase for the world of consumer electronics. We look forward to all of the technologies in our rainbow, and our attendees converging on Las Vegas (over 80,000 retail buyers, distributors, manufacturers, market analysts, importers/exporters and journalists from 112 countries attended last year).
