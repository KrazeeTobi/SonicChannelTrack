Bob Borchardt Remarks
 CEO, Recoton Corporation, CEMA Chairman
1997 Winter CES® - Opening Session

Thank you Gary for the kind introduction. I am honored to be here both as Chair of CEMA and as a member of the class of 1967.

The consumer electronics business has just ended one of the most difficult years I can ever remember. According to the analysts, consumers chose to use their discretionary income this holiday season for clothes, jewelry and toys. Consumer electronics and PCS suffered. What we all needed was an electronic Tickle Me Elmo.

I am confident the future holds great promise for sales and profits to rebound for the industry. How can I be so sure? Its simple. Consumer electronics products offer incredible value. What other type of product can you think of that has steadily decreased in price, while adding features and benefits. Virtually none.

Over the past 10 years, the cost of housing, apparel, automobiles and food have increased an average of three percent annually. On the other hand, prices of consumer electronics products, such as compact disc players, VCRs, camcorders and TVS have decreased by about four percent a year.

What is amazing to me is that during this same period we have unveiled so many great technological and feature advancements. For example, multi-disc CD players, VCRs that set the clock, picture in picture TV, wireless headphones, and Dolby Digital receivers. We continue to give consumers incredible products at very affordable prices. Value for value you just can't beat consumer electronics.

How is this all possible? First of all, the cost of these technologies has decreased dramatically over the years, so it does cost less to make many of these products. Secondly, both the manufacturers and retailers are in a constant battle for market share and keep taking drastic steps to grow their market position.

While, low prices are great for consumers, it has become detrimental to our industry. On the manufacturer side we have seen layoffs, plant closings or plant sell offs and mergers. On the retail side we have seen consolidation and store closings. We must not continue this way much longer if we expect to grow this industry further. An eye for an eye leaves two blind men. Maybe it is time for all of us to step back and regroup. Profitless prosperity is not the answer.

There is definitely room for optimism. The digital age is just around the corner and I believe that strong growth may be on the horizon. DVD and digital television should drive customers back into the stores. We have been anxiously awaiting DVD and consumers should finally be able to experience this outstanding technology in just a few months. While I do not expect DVD to be the savior for our industry, I anticipate that it will create some needed excitement and prompt consumers to upgrade other components such as their televisions, audio equipment and speakers.

As an industry, it is imperative that we take advantage of this opportunity and capitalize on the consumer's desire to create a true home theater experience. It is obvious from this past holiday selling season that people are willing to pay for quality products. We should fight to get our fair share of those dollars.

Just down the road is digital television. While the political process set us back just a bit, TV and computer manufacturers are already putting the pieces in place for a 1998 market introduction. I for one am looking forward to next January's CES where the first HDTV sets should be on display. Digital TV is a win-win for everyone -- manufacturers, retailers, broadcasters and consumer. I can't wait to get one in my home. For those that have not viewed this technology, I must tell you it is really incredible.

But, that isn't all. Flat screen TV, new cellular technology, digital radio and the proliferation of compact digital camcorders and cameras, are just some of the exciting new products and technologies that consumers can look forward to in the very near future. Look for them to flock to your stores.

We should take pride in our accomplishments and our technological advancements. We should not sell ourselves short or cheap. Our products are valuable and should not be taken for granted. Our New Year's resolution should be to not undervalue our products and to work together to make 1997 a very profitable and successful year. Notice the reoccurring message in my speech.

Leading the way to the future will be CEMA - our industry trade association. To many of you, CEMA is just the organization behind these great CE Shows. But, there is much more to this association. CEMA provides its members with numerous services and expertise ranging from research to lobbying.

Its extensive market research efforts provide us with monthly reports on over 500 product categories that help companies track sales to dealers. Additionally, CEMA conducts monthly consumer research projects that give us valuable feedback on consumers' likes, dislikes and future purchase intentions.

CEMA's technical experts coordinate efforts to create industry standards for new technologies. They have been instrumental in making sure we have future products like DVD, digital TV and digital audio radio. CEMA is also our voice in Washington, which, for many of our smaller members is their only voice. As an industry we can get more attention on Capitol Hill than we can as individuals and CEMA has done an excellent job in lobbying on our behalf.

CEMA recently played a major role in the HDTV negotiations, and has been assisting members in working through the complicated European Union directives that impact many smaller firms who export overseas.

I am pleased to announce some dramatic new CEMA programs for 1997. An industry Consumer Electronics Executive Summit to be held June 25-27 in Bermuda. This three-day invitation-only conference, which we hope will become a yearly event, will give senior level retailing and manufacturing executives a forum to discuss vital issues, take a look at the future and look towards growing a healthy consumer electronics industry. Issues to be addressed will include new technologies, returns, zero percent financing, and partnerships for the future.

Also planned for later in the year is a conference devoted solely to product returns, a serious problem that plagues both retailers and manufacturers. You will hear much more about both of these programs in the near future.

Lets reach out to a profitable future. None of us are alone out there. We should take advantage of the support and assistance CEMA provides. Together, we can all make 1997 a banner year. Good selling and have a great show.
