Bloomberg Says CE Industry Must Meet Consumer Needs

Media mogul Michael Bloomberg kicked off the 30th anniversary CES with a simple message to the industry: Provide the products consumers want, not the ones you want to sell. "I am a consumer. And consumers don't care what you want to sell," Bloomberg said in his keynote address. "They're the ones who are running this marketplace. Let's give them what they want."

What they want, he said, is to be able to access exactly the entertainment and information they want, when they want it. "The killer application is video on demand," he said. "You can't stop it, because the demand is there."

Bloomberg dismissed the much-hyped battle between the PC and the TV for the consumer's attention. "PC versus TV? Two years from now, no one's going to care," he said.

Bob Borchardt, CEMA chairman and CEO of Recoton, offered a sober assessment of the industry's health, but he predicted better times ahead.

"Consumer electronics just ended one of the most difficult years I can ever remember," Borchardt said. "Profitless prosperity is not the answer. Our New Year's resolution should be not to under-price our products."

Promising new technologies and products, however, can help pull the industry out of its current doldrums, Borchardt said. "Strong growth may be on the horizon with DVD and HDTV. Our industry must capitalize on the opportunity."

To address some of the challenges facing the industry, CEMA will host a summit of retail and manufacturing executives in Bermuda this June, Borchardt said. A separate CEMA conference, also set for this year, will address product returns.

As part of the opening ceremonies, CEMA president Gary Shapiro presented awards to 30 companies that have been exhibitors at each and every CES since 1967.
