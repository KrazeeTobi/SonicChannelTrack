Gary Shapiro Remarks
 CEMA President
1997 Winter CES® - Opening Session

Good morning and welcome to the 1997 Winter Consumer Electronics Show. Thirty years ago, the first CES® was held in New York City. We have special guests today who were at that first show. Can you please stand. We recognize your longevity in a tough but exciting industry.

The first CES was less than one-tenth of our size today. One hundred companies exhibited and 17,000 people attended.

The CES and our industry have grown together. The players have changed, but our philosophy has remained consistent. The CES must serve the industry by bringing all the players together in a cost effective format. And this format must be open to new players.

At the first association board meeting I attended in 1981, board chairman Ray Gates of Panasonic, then the largest CES exhibitor, expressed this philosophy during a discussion on whether we should increase the charge to exhibitors. He said, and the board agreed, that we must maintain the CES so a person with an idea can come to the CES and for a few thousand dollars expose a new product to thousands of retailers and media.

Since then, the entrepreneur has had a special place at CES and that's why we keep the CES focused on new products and technologies. We have a special pavilion for emerging companies; the Innovations exhibit at the Sands features the industry's most creative products and special pavilions for internet and intelligent transportation systems.

And because we are open to those with good ideas, our industry has always had significant growth and revolutionary products and technologies.

We have seen the VCR, camcorder, CD, pagers, cellular phone, fax machine, home computer and other products burst forth at CES like crops from a fertile field. During these past thirty years our industry has coined phrases that are now an everyday part of the American lexicom, such as home theater, multimedia and home office.

These products have changed the way we work, play and communicate. They have evolved from a luxury purchase to a household necessity. Can anyone imagine life without personal computers, camcorders, and VCRs? I certainly cannot, and our children certainly know of no other lifestyle.

In 1967 many of these products didn't exist, not even on the drawing boards. But, we had visions and visionaries. Akio Morita, K. Matsushita and Andre Blay are among the people who brought us the VCR, the Walkman and prerecorded videotape. Now there are new names and faces...Bill Gates, Andy Grove, Larry Ellison, Stan Hubbard, Eddy Hartenstein and Joe Clayton. These leaders are playing an integral role in the development and marketing of new technologies and innovations.

Over these past 30 years, the consumer electronics industry has thrived and prospered. In 1967, industry sales were 4.3 billion dollars. We have flourished to become a 65 billion dollar industry. That is a remarkable increase of more than fifteen hundred percent in 30 years.

While it is fun to reminisce, we must to look ahead. What will the world of consumer electronics look like in five years? What products will be introduced at the 2002 Winter CES? Maybe some brilliant visionaries and product developers know, but I certainly don't. What I do know, is these products - whatever they may be - will continue to improve our lives.

What we need to remember is that we are selling products, not hype. Every year, this industry conjures up a new buzz word and often they fly out as quickly as they flew in. This year, the buzz word is convergence. A couple of years ago we were all abuzz about the super highway - how we would receive telephone over cable and movies over phone lines. But, While everyone was talking abut this road to the future, DBS snuck in and paved the way. This real product has been one of the true successes for this industry. According to a survey we are releasing shortly, is dramatically cutting into cable revenue. In fact, our survey shows that 64 percent of DSS subscribers drop cable totally.

So, my advice is to not accept blindly the current view of technology Today many of the technologically vogue believe that the PC will replace the television as the focal point of the home entertainment system. I disagree. But ultimately consumers will make this choice.

They will decide which product is easiest to use, provides the best services and the most benefits, and offers the top value.

While I believe there are enough eyeballs to go around for the television and computer industries to benefit, we must continue to create exciting and compelling products. HDTV and DVD are two such products. Separately and together, HDTV and DVD will give consumers the entertainment experience they now only dream about.

Computers and televisions will continue to converge and evolve into new and exciting entertainment and productivity products. This is why we have teamed up with Softbank to bring together the Spring CES and Spring Comdex shows in Atlanta in June.

Your trade association, like its members, must be willing to push the envelope to think in non-traditional ways. We need your help in this effort.

Indeed, to get a different perspective we have created a consumer electronics manufacturers advisory council, comprised of some 50 leaders of national organizations such as the American Red Cross, the 4-H, the American Federation of Teachers, the Humane Society and several other groups. These CEO's are with us in Las Vegas this week to learn about our industry, to share their thoughts and hopefully, to give us insights to fulfill our obligation to you - to make the best industry even better.

But, before we look too far ahead, lets not forget about the reason we are all here. When you go on to the show floor in a few minutes, you will see over eighteen hundred exhibits and thousands of exciting products. In fact, this year's show is over 100,000 square feet larger than last year's show. As you walk the aisles, keep in mind that these are the products that will help grow this business. These are the products that will drive customers into your stores. These are the products that will take us all into the 21st century.

I am especially pleased to turn the microphone over to CEMA's top volunteer. One week ago, Bob Borchardt began his term as chair of CEMA's Board and Executive Committee As CEO of a leading American consumer electronics company, Bob ascendency to this position is a tribute to fundamental changes at CEMA, tremendous growth and success at Recoton and Bob's powerful vision and leadership. CEMA has tripled its membership in six years and has focused on serving the needs of American companies, large and small, in the consumer electronics industry. Bob Borchardt has been a vocal advocate of this focus and growth.

Bob has spent the past 36 years with Recoton. During his reign, Recoton has acquired 11 other firms including Discwasher and is now the industry's largest accessory company. This year, Recoton entered the audio business by acquiring International Jensen and its many highly recognized brands. Recoton currently manufacturers and markets 18 brand names with over 3,700 products. Ladies and gentleman, please welcome my friend, CEMA chairman and Reconton CEO, Bob Borchardt.
